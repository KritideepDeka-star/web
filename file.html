CONSTITUTION OF INDIA

  • History of the making of Indian Constitution

There are several incidents traced down in our indian history starting from the capture and establishment of British empire and the East India Company to the downfall and to the point where they finally left us. They were mentioned below accordingly.

    1)Historical Background
  • The British came to India in 1600 as traders in the form of East India Company, which had exclusive right of trading in India under a charter granted by Queen Elizabeth.
  • In 1765, EIC obtained ‘diwani’ (ie rights over revenue & justice) of Bengal, Bihar & Orissa.
  • The Company Rule (1773-1858).
  • The Crown Rule (1858-1947).

      • 2)The Company Rule (1773-1858)

      Regulating Act of 1773:

    Great Constitutional Importance as:

  • First step taken by the British Govt. to control & regulate affairs of EIC in India.
  • It recognised for the first time, the political & administrative functions of company.
  • It laid the foundations of central administration in India.

      • Features of the act:

  • ‘Governor of Bengal’ designated as the ‘Governor General of Bengal’.
  • 1st Governor General was Lord Warren Hastings.
  • Made Governor of Bombay & Madras presidencies subordinate.
  • Provided for Establishment of a Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774).
  • Prohibition on accepting presents or bribes.

      • Pitt's India Act(1784)

    Significant for two reasons:

  • Company’s territories in India were for the first time called the “British possessions in India”.
  • British Government was given the supreme control over Company’s affairs and its administration in India.

      • Charter's Act (1833)

    Features:

  • ➢A separate Governor General’s Legislative Council was established known as the Indian (Central) Legislative Council
  • ➢Introduced an open competition system of selection and recruitment of civil servants.
  • Civil Service Exam was made open to Indians.
  • Macaulay Committee on the ICS Exam was appointed in 1854.

      • The Crown Rule (1858-1947):

      Government of India Act of 1858:

  • Enacted as a result of the Revolt of 1857 (“1st War of Independence” or “Sepoy Mutiny”)
  • The Act abolished the East India Company
  • Transferred the powers of Government, Territories & Revenues to the British Crown.

    • Features of GOIA,1858:

  • Designation of “Governor General of India” was changed to “Viceroy of India”
  • Viceroy- Direct representative of the British Crown in India. Lord Canning- 1st Viceroy of India
  • Ended the system of double government by abolishing the Board of Control & Court of Directors
  • . It created a new office, “Secretary of State for India”, vested with complete authority & control over Indian Administration.“Secretary of State” was a member of British Cabinet & was responsible ultimately to the British Parliament.
  • It established a 15 member Council of India (advisory body) to assist the Secretary of State, who was its Chairman.
  • Constituted “the secretary of state in council” as a body corporate, capable of suing and being sued in India and in England.

      • Drafting Committee of the Indian Constitution

  • The Drafting Committee was formed on 29 August 1947 by the Constituent Assembly of India. Its main task was to draft the Constitution of independent India. The committee had 7 members, with Dr. B.R. Ambedkar as its Chairman.

      • Members of the Drafting Committee:

  • 1. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar – Chairman
  • 2. Alladi Krishnaswamy Iyer
  • 3. Alladi Krishnaswamy Iyer
  • 4.K.M. Munshi
  • 5.Mohammad Saadullah
  • 6.B.L. Mitter (resigned later, replaced by N. Madhava Rau)
  • 7. D.P. Khaitan (passed away, replaced by T.T. Krishnamachari)

      • Key Roles:

  • The committee prepared the first draft of the Constitution, discussed every article in detail, and presented the final draft to the Constituent Assembly, which adopted it on 26 November 1949.
  • The Indian Constitution came into effect on 26 January 1950

      • Working of Constituent Assembly: 9TH December, 1946 (First meeting)

  • Muslim League boycotted demanding Pakistan. Only 211 members attended. Dr Sachchidanand Sinha was elected Temporary President (oldest member)

      • 11th December, 1946.
      Dr Rajendra Prasad was elected President

  • HC Mukherjee elected Vice President. BN Rau appointed as Constitutional Advisor.

  • Resolve to proclaim India as an Independent Sovereign Republic.
  • Wherein shall be guaranteed and secured to all justice, social, economic and political; equality of status of opportunity and before law; freedom of thought, expression, belief faith, worship, vocation, association and action, subject to law and public morality.

      • Salient Features:

  • 1. Lengthiest Written Constitution Orginally (1949), the COI contained a Preamble, 395 Articles (divided into 22 Parts) & 8 Schedules

  • 2. Drawn from Various Sources:

  • 3. Blend of Rigidity & Flexibility: COI is neither rigid nor flexible but a synthesis of both.Article 368 provides for two types of amendments:
    a. Some provisions can be amended by a special majority of Parliament(2/3rd majority of members present of each house) and a majority(more than 50%) of total membership of each house.
    b. Someotherprovisions can be amended by a Special majority of the Parliament and with the ratification by half of the total states.
    Some provisions can be amended by simple majority of the Parliament in the manner of ordinary legislative process.

  • 4. Federal System with Unitary Bias: COI is described as “Federal in Form but Unitary in Spirit.”
    Article 1 describes India as Union of States.
    It contains all the features of a Federation viz. 2 governments, division of power, written constitution, bicameralism

  • 5. Parliamentary Form of Government: (Westminster model) British Parliamentary System of Government.
    a. Presence of nominal & real executives.
    b. Majority party rule.
    c. Collective responsibility of executive to legislature.
    d. Membership of ministers in the legislature.
    e. Leadership of PM or CM.

  • 6. Synthesis of Parliamentary Sovereignty & Judicial Supremacy: Supreme Court can declare parliamentary laws as unconstitutional through its power of Judicial Review.
    Parliament can amend the major portion of the Constitution through its constituent power.

  • 7. Integrated & Independent Judiciary: Single system of courts enforces both the central laws as well as the state laws.

  • 8. Fundamental Rights: (Justiciable in nature) Part III of the Indian Constitution guarantees 6 fundamental rights to all the citizens.
    a. Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)
    b. Right to Freedom (Articles 19-22)
    c. Right against Exploitation (Articles 23-24)
    d. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25-28)
    e. Cultural & Educational Rights (Articles 29-30)
    f.Right to Constitutional Remedies (Articles 32)
    These rights are enforceable by the courts for their violation.

  • 9. Directive Principles of State Policy: (Non- Justiciable in nature) (Part IV of the Constitution)
    Meant for promoting the ideal of social and economic democracy.
    They are not enforceable by courts for their violation.

  • 10. Fundamental Duties: (Non- Justiciable in nature) Added by 42nd Amendment, 1976 on recommendation of Swaran Singh Committee. 86th Constitutional Amendment act, 2002 added one more fundamental duty. Part IV-A (Article 51-A) specifies the 11 fundamental duties.
    To respect the Constitution, national flag, national anthem.
    To protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity of the country.
    To promote spirit of common brotherhood amongst all the people.
    To preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture and so on.

  • 11. A Secular State: The term “secular” was added to the Preamble by 42nd Constitutional Amendment of 1976.

  • 12. Universal Adult Franchise: Every citizen who is not less than 18 years of age has a right to vote without any discrimination of caste, race, religion, sex, literacy, wealth, so on.

  • 13. Single Citizenship

  • 14. Independent Bodies: Election Commission,
    Comptroller & Auditor General of India (CAG).
    Union Public Service Commission (UPSC).
    State Public Service Commissions (eg. APSC).

  • 15. Emergency Provisions: 3 Types:
    a. National Emergency on the ground of war or external aggression or armed rebellion (A.352)
    b. State Emergency (President’s Rule) on the ground of failure of Constitutional machinery in the state in the states (A.356) or failure to comply with the directions of the Centre (A.365)
    c. Financial Emergency on the ground of threat to the financial stability or credit of India (A.360)

  • 16. Three Tier Government: 73rd Amendment Act, 1992 gave Constitutional recognition to the panchayats (rural local governments) by adding a new Part IX & a newSchedule 11.
    74th Amendment Act, 1992 gave Constitutional recognition to the municipalities (urban local governments) by adding a new Part IX-A & a newSchedule 12.
    i.e. Union Government, State Government, Local Governments (panchayats/municipalities)
